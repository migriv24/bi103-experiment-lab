01 The Question

Learning Outcome: Science Skills

Science skills are the process behind a discovery: asking a clear question, forming a hypothesis, choosing variables, controlling for everything else, and deciding how to measure the result. This piece is that process on paper. The point is to show a sound experiment can be designed, not to run it.

Does adding two short lower-body strength sessions per week make my easy runs faster, without running any extra miles?

I already run easy, mellow miles (the Soft Running piece covers that). The natural next question is whether a small change, lifting twice a week, could make those same easy runs quicker at the same effort.

02 Background & Theory

A good experiment stands on something already known. The relevant idea here is running economy: how much energy you burn to hold a given pace. Two runners at the same speed can spend very different amounts of energy, and the more economical one can go faster for the same effort.

Endurance research has found that strength training improves running economy in trained runners, mostly through neuromuscular adaptations and stiffer, more efficient tendons rather than through bigger muscles. That is the mechanism this experiment leans on.

Grounding source

Ronnestad, B. R., & Mujika, I. (2014). Optimizing strength training for running and cycling endurance performance: A review. Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports, 24(4), 603–612.

03 Hypothesis

If I add two lower-body strength sessions per week for six weeks while keeping my running the same, then my average pace at an easy effort will get faster, because stronger, more efficient legs use less energy to hold the same speed.

Null hypothesis: the strength training makes no measurable difference to my easy pace.

04 The Variables

The whole design comes down to changing one thing and holding everything else still.

Independent

Strength training

The one thing I change: two lower-body sessions per week (squats, lunges, calf raises, hip work).

Dependent

Easy-run pace

What I measure: average pace at a fixed easy effort, with resting heart rate and perceived effort as backups.

Controlled

Everything else

Held steady: weekly mileage, same route, same morning time, sleep, and no big diet changes.

What gets trained

The strength work targets the muscles that drive running: quads, hamstrings, glutes, and calves. These are the same regions that lit up hottest in the Soft Running scan, which is part of why this experiment follows naturally from it.

05 The Protocol

A simple before-and-after design on a single person (me), run over six weeks.

Week 0

Baseline

Measure my easy pace across several normal runs and record resting heart rate. No strength training yet.

Weeks 1–6

Intervention

Keep running exactly the same, and add the two strength sessions each week. Keep logging every run.

End

Re-test

Measure easy pace and resting heart rate again under the same conditions, then compare to baseline.

How I would measure it

Pace and heart rate come from my Galaxy Watch, the same device behind the Data Management Lab, logged run by run. This experiment could fold straight into the body data I keep collecting for the course.

06 Predicted Results & Limits

If the hypothesis holds, I expect a small but real improvement: a few seconds per mile faster at the same easy effort, and possibly a slightly lower heart rate at the same pace. The change should be modest, since six weeks is short and these are gentle runs.

Confounds to watch

Weather, motivation, weight changes, and simply getting more used to the route could all nudge my pace on their own. Any of them could fake an effect or hide a real one.

Limitations

This is a single person with no control group and no blinding, so it cannot prove cause and effect on its own. It can only show a trend worth a bigger, better-controlled study.

Safety & ethics

The variable being tested is healthy by design. Strength training is good for me, done with proper form and rest, and there is no risky dieting or extreme load. This follows the assignment's guidance to test changes that should improve health.

Portfolio: Science Skills

  • Question to hypothesis: A clear, testable question becomes an if/then/because hypothesis tied to a known mechanism.
  • Variables: One independent variable, a measurable dependent variable, and a real list of controls.
  • Protocol & measurement: A baseline, an intervention, and a re-test, all measured the same way.
  • Honesty: The design names its own confounds and limits instead of hiding them.
